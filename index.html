Explore Wasp Phylogeny

Wasps are fascinating insects with species ranging from solitary to eusocial.

Wasps, which belong to the insect family Vespidae, include a diverse range of solitary and social behaviors (Hunt 2007). These social behaviors can include aggregation during feeding, parental care of the young, and communal nesting.

Eusociality, or “True Sociality”, is distinguished from other types of sociality in insects for species that exhibit these four characteristics:

  • Share a nest
  • Conspecific individuals cooperate in brood care
  • Reproductive division of labor, where sterile (or less fecund) individuals work for the benefit of a few reproductive individuals
  • Overlapping generations, where offspring contribute to nest/colony labor while parents are still alive

Among the wasps, yellowjackets are one group of eusocial insects, meeting all four of these criteria. Another common wasp, the paper wasp, is often considered semisocial because although there is reproductive division of labor, workers are not sterile as they are, for example, in yellowjackets or honeybees. Yet, these individuals typically forego their own reproductions for the colony. In comparison, solitary wasps, like mud daubers, create nests with prey items to provision their offspring but there is no overlap of generations in these wasps.

New Molecular Phylogeny Supports Two Independent Origins

Until rather recently, it was thought that eusociality in the wasps had evolved only once (Piekarski et al. 2018). This single origin of eusociality in wasps was derived from extensive character-based evidence (e.g., Carpenter 1982, Pickett & Carpenter 2010). However, mounting molecular evidence firmly places two independent origins of eusociality in wasps.

Because the social traits do not always map to the molecular phylogeny, the purpose of this site is to allow exploration of how key solitary and social traits may have evolved. It it our hope to spark new questions and hypotheses about the evolution of sociality in the context of the wasp phylogeny.

Who Are We?


We are an international consortium of social insect researchers spanning three countries: the USA, UK, and Canada. Our interests lie at the intersection of genomics, behavior, phylogeny, and physiology. You can read more about our individual interests at our websites.
